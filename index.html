ORD-WM: Learning Object-Centric Rigid Dynamics for 3D World Modeling in Robotic Manipulation

Anonymous Authors
arXiv Code

In contrast to video-based rollout and point-based scene-flow prediction, which may suffer from visual artifacts, shape drift, or object-level inconsistency, ORD-WM performs multi-step prediction by analytically applying predicted object-wise SE(3) updates to geometry-aware object latents composed of 3D vector features, preserving object geometry while updating pose. This latent-space rollout enables real-world object motion prediction and closed-loop model predictive control (MPC), where candidate actions are evaluated by their predicted effects on object states.

Abstract

World models provide robots with a predictive mechanism for reasoning about how actions change object states in manipulation tasks. However, existing image-based and dense 3D world models do not explicitly impose object-level rigid motion, leaving rigid-object geometry preservation to be learned implicitly and making multi-step rollouts susceptible to shape drift or object-level motion inconsistency. To address this, we propose ORD-WM, a 3D world model that learns object-centric rigid dynamics for robotic manipulation. ORD-WM learns an SDF-supervised vector-neuron variational autoencoder whose encoder maps each object point cloud into a geometry-aware latent representation composed of 3D vector features, and predicts action-conditioned object-wise SE(3) motion increments. Rather than generating unconstrained next latent states, ORD-WM analytically applies predicted object-wise rigid transformations to the current latent states, separating persistent object geometry from pose changes. Experiments show that ORD-WM enables stable, geometry-preserving multi-step prediction and supports efficient closed-loop model predictive control in both simulation and real-world manipulation scenarios.

ORD-WM delivers strong results across simulation and real-world robotic manipulation benchmarks. It achieves the best scratch performance on LIBERO, competitive long-horizon performance on CALVIN ABC→D, and consistently outperforms Diffusion Policy and OpenVLA on real-world tasks. These results show that video-based world priors can be effectively distilled into a lightweight action policy without sacrificing performance.

What is actually distilled?

The distilled action latent aligns more consistently with task-relevant regions in the teacher video latent, such as the gripper and manipulated objects. This suggests that action generation mainly relies on features from task-relevant and highly dynamic regions, while background regions are largely ignored during distillation. Notably, the highlighted regions also correspond well to task-relevant areas in the future images, suggesting that the action DiT captures future-aware world priors rather than merely reflecting the current observation. In contrast, the heatmaps read out from the baseline do not exhibit consistent similarity patterns, indicating that ORD-WM truly transfers latent knowledge from the video DiT into the action DiT, rather than merely allowing the Q-Former to fit the video latent.

Inference efficiency

A key goal of ORD-WM is to retain the benefit of video-based world priors without incurring the overhead of an explicit video model at test time. To verify this, we compare inference latency across representative baselines on the same hardware platform, a compute node equipped with a single NVIDIA A100 40GB GPU. ORD-WM achieves the lowest average inference latency among all compared methods, requiring only 55\,ms per action prediction. In contrast, mimic-video, a WAM that retains explicit future imagination, incurs about 21X higher latency. This efficiency gain comes from discarding the video DiT entirely at inference time and deploying only an action model. Moreover, with a lightweight text encoder, ORD-WM requires only about 6 GB of GPU memory at deployment, which is an advantage in compute-constrained settings.

we compare ORD-WM with two simplified variants: w/o distillation, which removes the distillation objective and trains the action DiT only with the flow-matching objective, and w/o PCA, which directly uses the original teacher latent without feature compression. Both variants perform worse than the full model. Removing distillation leads to a clear performance drop, confirming that the compact teacher video latents provide useful supervision beyond standard action modeling. Meanwhile, directly distilling the uncompressed teacher latent also degrades performance, suggesting that the raw teacher features are overly high-dimensional and noisy for effective transfer. These results show that both the proposed distillation design and PCA-based compression are important for transferring control-relevant world priors into the policy.
